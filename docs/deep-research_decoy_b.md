Advanced Computational Frameworks for Characterizing TCR–pMHC Interfaces and Predicting Structural Cross-Reactivity in Immunotherapy ScreeningThe development of T-cell receptor (TCR) engineered T-cell therapies (TCR-T) is a paradigm-shifting approach in oncology, yet its clinical implementation is fraught with the risk of lethal off-target toxicities. The fundamental challenge lies in the dual nature of T-cell recognition: the immune system requires TCRs to be highly specific for cognate antigens while maintaining enough cross-reactivity (poly-specificity) to cover the vast universe of potential pathogens with a limited repertoire of T-cells. A single TCR is estimated to recognize between $10^4$ and $10^7$ distinct peptide-major histocompatibility complex (pMHC) ligands. Consequently, the success of therapeutic TCR design depends on the ability to predict which self-peptides presented on human leukocyte antigens (HLA) might trigger a cross-reactive response. Current screening systems often rely on sequence homology or simple backbone deviations, such as C$\alpha$ Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD), but these metrics are notoriously poor proxies for the intricate physicochemical and geometric complementarity that defines TCR triggering.This report examines state-of-the-art computational descriptors and scoring features for protein-protein interfaces (PPIs), specifically focusing on the TCR–pMHC interaction. It details how these descriptors can be integrated into a high-throughput structural decoy scoring pipeline to identify potential off-target liabilities in a therapeutic context. The analysis moves beyond sequence-level similarities to investigate the structural "immunological fingerprints" that drive molecular mimicry and cross-recognition.The Biophysical Landscape of the TCR–pMHC InterfaceThe recognition of a pMHC by a TCR is the critical first step in adaptive immunity, yet the biophysical parameters governing this interaction are distinct from those seen in other protein-protein interactions, such as antibody-antigen or enzyme-substrate complexes. TCR–pMHC interfaces are generally characterized by a relatively flat topography, a low density of hydrogen bonds, and a heavy reliance on van der Waals (VDW) interactions and hydrophobic packing. Natural TCR–pMHC interactions typically exist in a low-affinity regime, with dissociation constants ($K_d$) ranging from 1 to 100 $\mu M$. This contrasts sharply with the nanomolar or picomolar affinities achieved by affinity-matured antibodies or engineered TCR mimics (TCRms).Topology and Docking GeometryThe TCR binds to the pMHC in a characteristic diagonal orientation. In Class I MHC complexes, the TCR variable domains typically bind at a $35^\circ$ angle across the complex's major axis, while in Class II complexes, the angle increases to approximately $50^\circ$. The TCR $\alpha$-chain variable domain ($V\alpha$) is generally centered over the MHC $\alpha_2$-helix, and the $\beta$-chain variable domain ($V\beta$) primarily contacts the MHC $\alpha_1$-helix. The specificity of the interaction is largely dictated by the hypervariable complementarity-determining region 3 (CDR3) loops, which position themselves directly over the central residues of the MHC-bound peptide.Recent studies indicate that the "docking footprint" of a TCR on the pMHC is a critical determinant of signaling outcome. Outlier binding geometries, where the TCR deviates significantly from the canonical diagonal mode, often lead to weak or non-existent signaling, possibly due to poor alignment of the TCR–CD3 complex for downstream intracellular force-sensing.Interaction Hotspots and Energetic PartitioningTCR–pMHC interfaces contain "hotspots"—localized regions that provide the majority of the binding free energy. Structural analysis of non-redundant TCR–pMHC complexes reveals that interaction residues within 5 $\text{\AA}$ of the peptide are concentrated in the CDR3 loops, specifically at IMGT positions 107–116. In approximately 90% of observed structures, a linear cluster of 3–5 amino acids in the CDR3$\beta$ loop serves as the primary contact hub.Energetic footprinting using surface plasmon resonance (SPR) on mutant pMHC libraries suggests that residues within these hotspots contribute additively to the total binding energy. However, different TCRs restricted to the same HLA allele (e.g., HLA-A*02:01) utilize distinct energetic footprints. For instance, while some TCRs rely heavily on peptide residues p4 and p5, others derive significant stabilization from MHC-alpha helix residues such as R65, K66, or W167. Identifying these specific TCR interaction profiles is essential for predicting cross-reactivity, as cross-recognition often hinges on the preservation of these specific hotspot contacts even when the rest of the peptide sequence varies significantly.Interface Descriptors and Feature SetsTo build a robust safety screening pipeline, it is necessary to move beyond backbone RMSD and utilize descriptors that capture the detailed side-chain landscape and physicochemical environment of the interface.Geometric FeaturesGeometric complementarity is the baseline requirement for binding. Buried Surface Area (BSA) or $\Delta$SASA (the change in solvent-accessible surface area upon complexation) is a standard proxy for the hydrophobic effect at the interface. For TCR–pMHC complexes, BSA values are typically lower than those of antibody-antigen complexes, reflecting the flatter nature of the interface.Shape complementarity ($S_c$), defined as a value between 0 and 1, quantifies how well the geometric surfaces of the TCR loops and the pMHC pack against each other. While $S_c$ is a highly descriptive feature, it is exceptionally sensitive to side-chain modeling uncertainty in predicted structures; a 1 $\text{\AA}$ shift in a side-chain rotamer can lead to steric clashes or artificial gaps that significantly degrade the $S_c$ score. Higher-order descriptors, such as interface curvature and gap volume index, further characterize the "looseness" or "tightness" of the interaction.Physicochemical and Energetic FeaturesThe thermodynamic stability of the interface is governed by the per-residue interaction energy decomposition. This involves partitioning the binding free energy into terms such as VDW attraction/repulsion, Coulombic electrostatics, hydrogen bonding, and desolvation.Electrostatic surface potential is a particularly important feature for TCR recognition, as many cross-reactive mimics utilize charge complementarity to overcome sequence divergence. Rosetta’s "dG_separated" and "fa_elec" (full-atom electrostatics) terms are examples of hybrid potentials that combine physical force fields with statistical observations. These potentials are often more robust than pure physics-based methods because they implicitly account for the "average" effects of solvent and side-chain flexibility.Interaction FingerprintsInteraction fingerprints (IFPs) provide a high-level representation of non-covalent contacts. Tools like the Protein-Ligand Interaction Profiler (PLIP) and Arpeggio map the interface into a vector of discrete interaction types (H-bonds, salt bridges, $\pi$-stacking, etc.). The similarity between two pMHC surfaces can then be calculated using the Tanimoto coefficient or other set-similarity metrics on these fingerprints. This approach is particularly powerful for identifying molecular mimics that share a common "interaction profile" despite having highly divergent primary sequences.TCR–pMHC Specific Features and Modeling ToolsSeveral specialized tools and datasets have emerged for predicting TCR binding and cross-reactivity, often integrating sequence data with structural insights.TCR-pMHC Specific Prediction ModelsThe first generation of models, such as NetTCR and NetTCR-2.2, utilized convolutional neural networks (CNNs) trained primarily on sequence data to predict binding. While effective within their training domains, these models often fail to generalize to "unseen" peptides not present in the training set.The second generation of models incorporates structural information more explicitly:TITAN: Uses a multimodal approach, integrating TCR and peptide sequences with MHC allele information.ERGO-2: Employs long short-term memory (LSTM) networks and autoencoders to learn latent representations of TCR and peptide sequences.NetTCR-struc: A structure-aware GNN model that reranks poses generated by AlphaFold-Multimer. It achieved a 25% increase in Spearman's correlation with DockQ scores (a metric of docking quality) compared to native AlphaFold metrics.STAG (Structural TCR And pMHC Graph): A graph neural network that represents each residue as a node and uses radial basis functions (RBF) to encode distance-based edges between them. STAG is specifically designed to leverage local 3D structural characteristics that sequence-based methods miss.t2pmhc: A structure-informed GNN framework designed to solve the generalization problem for unseen antigens. By focusing attention on the peptide and CDR3 regions and down-weighting MHC anchor residues, t2pmhc identifies the biophysical determinants of binding more reliably than sequence-based models.Characterizing Molecular MimicryMolecular mimicry is the phenomenon where a foreign peptide resembles a host self-peptide sufficiently to trigger a cross-reactive response. Quantitatively, mimicry is defined not by sequence identity but by structural and electrostatic similarity in the MHC-presented state.Studies using the MimicryDB-Auto dataset demonstrate that sequence similarity alignment scores (like BLAST or BLOSUM80) have almost no predictive utility for identifying structural mimics in short 8–15 residue MHC-presented peptides. Instead, cross-reactivity is better predicted by "ensemble molecular mimicry" (EMM), which considers the thermodynamic similarity between pMHC complexes modeled as conformational ensembles. Epitopes that share a "pairwise thermodynamic signature" can be cross-reactive even in the complete absence of detectable sequence or global structural similarity.High-Throughput Computational Tools and SoftwareA structure-based screening pipeline requires a suite of tools capable of processing thousands of candidate structures with high efficiency and accuracy.Structural Generation and RefinementThe initial generation of 3D models from sequence has been transformed by the release of AlphaFold2, AlphaFold3, and specialized variants.AlphaFold3 (AF3): The current state-of-the-art for predicting multimeric protein-protein complexes. AF3 achieves a median DockQ score > 0.6 for TCR–pMHC complexes, a significant improvement over previous methods. A key insight for safety screening is that the pLDDT confidence score of the TCR CDR3 region is a highly informative indicator of structural correctness and mutation-induced affinity changes.TCRmodel2 & ColabFold: These tools provide a 70-fold acceleration in structural prediction without substantial loss in accuracy, making them ideal for screening massive decoy libraries.Boltz-1 & Boltz-2: New replication models of AF3 architecture that support "Boltz-steering" to improve physical plausibility and avoid steric clashes.Interface Analysis and ScoringOnce structures are generated, they must be analyzed using specialized scoring engines.FoldX (AnalyseComplex): Highly optimized for high-throughput mutational screening. It is best used for calculating $\Delta \Delta G_{bind}$ between the wild-type target and decoy mutants.Rosetta InterfaceAnalyzer: Provides the most detailed decomposition of interface descriptors, including hydrogen bonding, solvation, and packing scores. For predicted models with potential structural strain, the "relax" and "cartesian_ddg" protocols are essential to prevent artificial energy penalties.PRODIGY: A contact-based binding affinity predictor that utilizes the number and type of interfacial contacts (charged/polar/apolar). PRODIGY is exceptionally fast, with an average runtime of 3–5 seconds per structure, making it a powerful triage filter.PeSTo (Protein Structure Transformer): A parameter-free geometric deep learning model that predicts interaction interfaces in approximately 300ms. PeSTo eliminates the "overhead" of pre-computing molecular surfaces, making it robust and generalizable across all molecule types.Flexibility and DynamicsTCR–pMHC recognition is a dynamic process. Static crystal structures (and their AI-predicted analogs) only provide a snapshot of the interface.MDAnalysis / ProDy: These Python-based libraries allow for the analysis of structural ensembles generated by molecular dynamics (MD) or normal mode analysis (NMA).RMSF and B-Factor Analysis: Root Mean Square Fluctuation (RMSF) serves as a surface flexibility proxy, indicating which regions of the pMHC are more likely to undergo induced-fit rearrangements upon TCR engagement.Integration Strategy for a Unified Scoring PipelineThe heterogeneous features extracted from geometric, physicochemical, and learned models must be synthesized into a single decision metric to rank potential off-target candidates.Hierarchical Virtual ScreeningA 10,000-member decoy library should be processed through a multi-stage filtering hierarchy to balance computational depth with throughput.Phase 1: Sequence-Based Pre-Filtering (All Candidates): Use tools like NetMHCpan-4.1 to estimate HLA-binding affinity. Candidates with a binding rank > 2% can be discarded as they are unlikely to be presented on the cell surface.Phase 2: Rapid Structural Generation (Hundreds of Candidates): Use TCRmodel2 or ColabFold to generate initial ensembles of TCR–pMHC complexes for the remaining candidates.Phase 3: Fast Feature Extraction (Triage): Run PeSTo, PRODIGY, and PLIP to identify candidates with interface profiles and contact patterns that mimic the cognate target.Phase 4: Deep Scoring and LTR (Finalists): Perform Rosetta energy decomposition and CDR3-pLDDT analysis on the top-ranking candidates. Use a Learning-to-Rank (LTR) model (e.g., XGBoost) to generate a final safety score.Learning-to-Rank (LTR) with Gradient Boosted TreesWeighted linear combinations (current approach) are often insufficient for modeling the non-linear thresholds of TCR triggering. A Learning-to-Rank framework, such as XGBoost or LightGBM, can be trained to distinguish between binders and "compelling decoy pairs".A specialized implementation, PPIscreenML, uses descriptors from AlphaFold confidence measures complemented by Rosetta energetic terms to achieve high accuracy in distinguishing true interactors from decoys. Similarly, training an XGBoost classifier on electrostatic potential patterns across 92 immunologically relevant ROIs achieved an F1 score of 0.92 in distinguishing viral from self-peptides.Training and Validation DatasetsThe efficacy of an LTR model depends on high-quality training data that includes balanced positive and negative examples.VDJdb & IEDB: The primary repositories for experimentally verified TCR-peptide pairings.STCRDab & TCR3D: The most comprehensive collections of experimentally resolved TCR structural data, essential for validating structural descriptors.ATLAS Database: Links high-resolution TCR–pMHC structures with corresponding binding affinity data ($K_d$ or $\Delta G$), which is critical for training energetic features.BATCAVE (Benchmark for Activation of T-cells): A newly developed database containing continuous-valued TCR activation data from deep mutational scans of 25 immunogenic epitopes. BATCAVE provides balanced true positive and true negative examples for over 100 TCRs, addressing the "negative data" gap in computational immunology.MimicryDB-Auto: A curated dataset of 399 pathogen-host peptide pairs spanning both MHC Class I and II, integrating sequence alignment with atomic structural validation to study molecular mimicry.Practical Constraints and Hardware InfrastructureTo meet the requirement of processing 500 candidates within seconds on a Linux GPU server, the pipeline must leverage GPU acceleration and parallelized execution.Robustness to Structural UncertaintyPredicted structures (tFold/AF3) inherently contain $\sim 1\text{--}2 \text{\AA}$ of backbone error, which can create artificial steric clashes in physics-based force fields. Descriptors derived from graph-based architectures (STAG, t2pmhc) and learned embeddings (ESM-IF1, HERMES) are significantly more robust to these errors because they rely on coarser representations, such as residue interaction networks or local density distributions, rather than precise atomic-level coordinates.GPU Optimization and ScalingCompute-intensive operations can be offloaded to the GPU to achieve massive throughput.MMseqs2-GPU: Rapidly computes multiple sequence alignments (MSAs) of protein sequences, achieving a 177x speedup over JackHMMER. Traditional sequence-based similarity and C$\alpha$ RMSD fail to capture the "mimicry paradox"—the reality that sequence-dissimilar peptides can converge on identical antigenic surfaces within the HLA groove.A robust scoring pipeline must prioritize the characterization of the hypervariable CDR3 regions, as they constitute the dominant energetic and geometric hotspot in over 90% of observed TCR–pMHC structures. For structural generation, AlphaFold3 is the current gold standard, but its internal confidence metrics must be supplemented with CDR3-specific pLDDT reranking and graph-based poses assessment tools like NetTCR-struc or STAG to handle the plasticity of the TCR loops.Furthermore, structural similarity should be assessed in the context of thermodynamic ensembles rather than static snapshots.
